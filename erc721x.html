ERC721X: an optimized erc721 implementation

Meta Penguin Island will be the lowest gas-cost mint ever in NFT history.

ERC721X is an implementation of ERC721 with significant gas savings for minting and transferring NFTs.

The number one priority of this initiative is to put users first and lessen the increasing burden of high gas costs.

Our aim is to influence other NFT projects to explore optimized contracts and innovate on behalf of their users.

We’ve hired 3 independent security auditors to review the soundness and efficiency of our approach.

Why ERC721X?

Ethereum prices

When communities at large begin to mint new projects simultaneously, the entire ecosystem spikes and results in extremely high gas. ERC721X is a response to mitigating these unnecessarily high transaction fees.

Because we care

As Meta Penguin Island prepares to enter the metaverse, we want to start things off on the right foot and raise the bar. Our goal is to have our community spend as little as possible in gas fees while minting.

Our team motto “no shortcuts”

Our entire team stands by this motto. We’ve dedicated ourselves to only producing the highest quality work across every aspect of Meta Penguin Island. This idea extends deep into development as we innovate with this new technology.

How does it work?

For those who are interested in the technical details that lie behind our ERC721X contract, we've outlined the 3 main optimizations. If you have experience with Solidity and OpenZeppelin's implementation of ERC721 and the Enumerable extension we encourage you to learn more about our approach below.

Optimization 1

Deferring the calculation of the balanceOf function

The widely-used OpenZeppelin implementation of ERC721 uses a mapping to keep track of the NFT balances of each account. This requires updating a value on every mint and transfer.

We removed this mapping to save the gas costs of the updates and instead defer the calculation of the balance to the balanceOf function itself.

Note: This makes the balanceOf function have to iterate through all the minted token IDs to calculate a result which indeed makes its gas cost very high. However, this can be overcome with off-chain computation where the smart contract is provided the IDs owned by an address and calculates its balance by validating those via ownerOf.

Optimization 2

Deferring the calculation of the Enumerable functions

OpenZeppelin’s implementation of the Enumerable extension contains extra storage writes for every mint and transfer. These are in place to reduce the costs of read functions at a significant cost to write functions. Additionally, for serially minted token IDs, some of the additional storage writes are plainly redundant.

We removed these storage writes and instead calculate the Enumerable functionality on the totalSupply, tokenByIndex & tokenOfOwnerByIndex functions.

Note: Although the other two functions are computed in O(1), tokenOfOwnerByIndex encounters the same issue of having to iterate all minted token IDs as balanceOf from the previous optimization. This can again be overcome in the same way via off-chain computation.

Optimization 3

Updating owners array once per mint

Traditional ERC721 implementations store a token ID to owner mapping to keep track of who owns what token. In the case of minting multiple tokens, this mapping must be updated multiple times, each time adding to the gas cost. Inspired by Azuki, ERC721x creates an ownership structure that implies ownership of two consecutive token IDs while only requiring 1 write to storage instead of 2.
